Let's step back now and talk about the methodological approach I am taking (and I should apologize, as many of you may be quite familiar with this already, but I've found it is wise to lay everything out clearly so we all understand exactly what is happening). The methodology I use is something know as stylometry, an idea originally developed for authorship identification. This is the idea that different people use words at different rates, often unconsiously. I can study variable frequencies in word usage to determine who may have written a document or to assess what document a sentence or fragment may have come from. The words that are most often useful for this are the most common ones: in english the rates at which the 40 most common words like 'the" "of and "is" are used can be predictive of authorship. In Chinese these most common words are often vey predictive of genre, but they also contain authorship signals. Scholars have used appraoches like this to establish authorship in cases of questional attribution, for example in the 1960s scholars reevaulateed the authorship of twelve of the Federalist Papers (anonymous essays encouraging support for the US consitution). More recently, people have shown that JK Rowling of Harry Potter fame wrote some detective fiction under a pseudonym

Vector Space Model

Document Vectors

Each document is represented by a list comprised of normalized word frequencies

This list of numbers can be understood as a vector, or a point in space. I have essentially spatialized my documents, creating what is known as a vector space model (a model being a simplified way of representing a system). Each document is now a point in space and I can simply measure the straight line distances between them. The shorter this distance, the more stylistically similar two documents are .

This might be a somewhat discomfiting defintion of style, moving away from a qualitative experience of the text to one that is quantitatively defined. I am using, a particular definition of style [next slide]

Style is a property of texts constituted by an ensemble of formal features which can be observed quantitatively or qualitatively.
